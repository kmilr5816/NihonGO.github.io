Katakana

Introduction

Katakana is the second writing system you'll learn when studying Japanese. It consists of 46 characters, each representing a distinct syllable—like "カ" (ka) or "タ" (ta). Unlike the English alphabet, where letters combine to form sounds, each katakana character already conveys a complete sound.

You'll use katakana primarily for foreign loanwords, onomatopoeia, and scientific or technical terms. It's also used for foreign names and brand names, and for emphasis—much like italics in English—so you'll see it in menus, signs, packaging, and advertising.

Learning katakana is the next step after hiragana. It unlocks your ability to read modern vocabulary as it appears in daily life, from コンピュータ (konpyūta, "computer") to ワンワン (wanwan, "woof woof"). Once you master it, you'll be able to recognize and pronounce a vast range of words you encounter in media, street signs, and product labels. Even at advanced stages, katakana remains essential for understanding new and borrowed terms.
